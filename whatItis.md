Build a clean, modern, mobile-responsive landing page for my iOS app. The goal of the page is to make the app look legitimate for App Store launch, explain what the app does clearly to early users, and provide all required public links like privacy, support, terms, and account deletion. The landing page should not feel like a huge corporate website, but it should feel polished, trustworthy, and real. It should have a simple header with the app logo or app name on the left and navigation links on the right: Features, How It Works, Support, Privacy, and optionally Download. The hero section should be the strongest part of the page: include the app name, a very clear one-line value proposition, a short supporting sentence explaining who the app is for and why it matters, a large app screenshot or phone mockup, and a primary call-to-action button such as “Download on the App Store” or “Coming Soon on the App Store.” If the app is not live yet, use a disabled/coming-soon App Store style button or a waitlist button only if we are actually collecting emails. The hero should immediately answer what the app is, who it helps, and why someone should care. After the hero, add a short problem section that explains the user pain in plain language, not buzzwords. Then add a “How It Works” section with three simple steps: first, the user imports/saves/adds something into the app; second, the app organizes, analyzes, or helps them act on it; third, the user gets the useful result/outcome. Each step should have a short title, one short paragraph, and a simple icon or visual. After that, add a features section with 4–6 feature cards maximum. Each card should have a short title, one clean sentence or small paragraph, and optionally an icon. Do not overwhelm the page with every tiny feature; focus only on the strongest core features. Add a screenshots section showing 2–5 app screens in phone mockups, ideally including the home screen, the main action screen, a details/results screen, and a settings/account/support screen if available. Add a “Who It’s For” section that explains the target users in simple terms so visitors can self-identify. Add a trust/support section that makes the app feel safe and legitimate: mention that support is available by email, user data is handled according to the privacy policy, and users can request account/data deletion. Do not invent fake testimonials or fake numbers. If there are no testimonials yet, do not include testimonials. Add a final call-to-action section near the bottom repeating the main CTA, like “Ready to get started?” with the App Store button or coming soon message. The footer must include the app name, a short one-line description, support email, copyright line, and links to Privacy Policy, Terms of Service, Support, and Delete Account. Required routes/pages should include: “/” for the main landing page, “/privacy” for the existing privacy policy, “/support” for support/contact instructions, “/terms” for terms of service, and “/delete-account” for account deletion instructions if the app has user accounts. The support page should clearly say how users can contact us for bugs, account issues, billing issues, privacy questions, or general help. The delete account page should explain exactly how users can request deletion, for example by emailing support from the email linked to their account with the subject “Delete my account,” unless there is already an in-app deletion flow. Visually, the site should be clean, premium, fast-loading, and optimized for mobile first. Use lots of spacing, clear hierarchy, rounded cards, subtle shadows or borders, and a modern app-style layout. Avoid huge walls of text, fake startup buzzwords, excessive animations, broken buttons, placeholder text, and any claims about features that are not actually in the app. The website should be easy for Apple reviewers and normal users to understand. Every link in the header/footer must work. The privacy policy link must be visible. The support link must be visible. The design should look good on both desktop and mobile, but mobile is especially important because users may open it from the App Store. The final page should feel like a legitimate app website with just enough marketing, clarity, and compliance links to support an App Store launch without overbuilding.
